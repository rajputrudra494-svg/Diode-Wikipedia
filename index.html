What is a Diode?

A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device that allows current to flow in one direction (forward bias) while blocking it in the reverse direction â€” acting as a one-way valve for electrical current.

ðŸ”¬

Working Principle

A diode consists of a P-type semiconductor (excess holes) and N-type semiconductor (excess electrons) joined together. At the junction, a depletion region forms creating a potential barrier. Forward bias reduces this barrier allowing current flow, while reverse bias widens it blocking current.

I-V Characteristics

The I-V curve shows exponential current rise beyond the threshold voltage (0.7V for Si, 0.3V for Ge) in forward bias. In reverse bias, only a tiny leakage current flows until the breakdown voltage is reached, where current increases rapidly.

Key Parameters

Forward Voltage (Vf): Minimum voltage for conduction. Reverse Breakdown (Vbr): Voltage at which reverse current surges. Maximum Current (If): Safe operating limit. Recovery Time: Switching speed from ON to OFF.

Biasing Modes

Forward Bias: Positive terminal â†’ P-side, Negative â†’ N-side. Barrier reduces, current flows. Reverse Bias: Connections reversed. Barrier widens. Zero Bias: No external voltage. Equilibrium state with no net current.

Circuit Symbol

The diode symbol is a triangle (anode) pointing toward a vertical line (cathode). Current flows from anode to cathode. The triangle indicates the direction of conventional current flow. The cathode is marked with a band on physical components.
